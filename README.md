# RATER — A Museum of Evaluation

RATER is a conceptual web exhibition that explores how humans assign value.  
Rather than functioning as a traditional product rating website, the project presents evaluation itself as a cultural artifact.

The site treats ranking systems, scoring frameworks, and collective opinion as museum objects. Through visual hierarchy, spatial design, and numerical symbolism, the interface demonstrates how societies construct ideas of taste, legitimacy, and prestige.

The project draws inspiration from Apple-style interface design and museum exhibition layouts, prioritizing clarity, whitespace, typography, and restrained motion.

## Concept

Modern culture is saturated with systems of ranking:  
product reviews, music scores, film ratings, follower counts, and algorithmic metrics.

These systems often claim objectivity but frequently reveal the preferences and biases of the cultures that create them.

RATER reframes rating systems as exhibits. Each section of the website presents a conceptual "artifact" that represents a different mechanism through which societies judge quality and value.

## Sections

The site contains several conceptual exhibits:

**The Ladder of Taste**  
Explores how social status and cultural signaling shape perceptions of “good taste.”

**Consensus Machine**  
Examines how aggregate ratings and review averages become perceived as objective truth.

**The Social Mirror**  
Investigates how individuals unconsciously adapt their opinions to match collective trends.

**The Curated Self**  
Looks at how modern digital identities are constructed for evaluation through followers, metrics, and algorithmic visibility.

## Design Philosophy

The site is designed as a digital exhibition space rather than a conventional interface.

Key design influences include:

- Apple.com product presentation
- modern museum exhibition layouts
- editorial typography
- restrained motion and spatial pacing

Large typography, generous spacing, and subtle scroll animations encourage visitors to read the site slowly, treating the experience as an exhibition rather than a browsing task.
